The History and Basic Rules of Chess

Introduction

Chess is one of the oldest and most enduring strategy games in human history, played today by hundreds of millions of people worldwide and recognized by the International Olympic Committee as a mind sport. Despite its modern global reach, the game's origins trace back over 1,500 years to a very different early form.

Historians generally trace chess's origins to a 6th-century Indian game called chaturanga, whose name refers to the four divisions of an ancient Indian army — infantry, cavalry, elephants, and chariotry — which evolved over centuries into the pieces recognizable in the modern game.

How Chess Traveled and Transformed

From India, chaturanga spread to Persia, where it became known as shatranj, and it was from the Persian term that the game eventually derived its modern name in many languages, including the English word 'chess.' After the Islamic conquest of Persia in the 7th century, shatranj spread rapidly across the Islamic world, and Muslim scholars significantly advanced chess theory and strategy during this period, writing some of the earliest known chess literature.

Chess reached Europe via Muslim Spain and the Byzantine Empire by around the 9th and 10th centuries, and underwent significant rule changes during the late 15th century in Europe, when the queen and bishop pieces gained dramatically increased mobility, transforming the game's pace and strategy into something much closer to the modern version played today.

How the Pieces Move

Each player begins with sixteen pieces: one king, one queen, two rooks, two bishops, two knights, and eight pawns. The king moves one square in any direction but is the most important piece, since the game ends immediately when a king is checkmated. The queen, the most powerful piece, moves any number of squares in any straight or diagonal direction.

Rooks move any number of squares horizontally or vertically; bishops move any number of squares diagonally, with each bishop permanently confined to squares of one color; and knights move in a distinctive L-shape, two squares in one direction and one square perpendicular, uniquely able to jump over other pieces. Pawns move forward one square at a time (two on their first move), capture diagonally, and can be promoted to any other piece upon reaching the far end of the board.

How the Game Is Won

The objective of chess is to checkmate the opponent's king, meaning the king is under direct attack (in 'check') and has no legal move to escape capture. A game can also end in a draw under several circumstances, including stalemate (a player has no legal moves but their king is not in check), the threefold repetition rule (the same position occurs three times), the fifty-move rule (fifty moves pass without a capture or pawn movement), or mutual agreement between players.

Unlike many games, chess has no element of chance — no dice, no shuffled cards — making it a game of complete information where both players can theoretically see every possible move, which is precisely why it has been studied so extensively by mathematicians, computer scientists, and, more recently, artificial intelligence researchers.

Chess in the Modern Computer Age

Chess became a landmark benchmark for artificial intelligence research throughout the 20th century, culminating in IBM's Deep Blue defeating world champion Garry Kasparov in 1997, a widely covered milestone often cited as a turning point in public perception of computer intelligence. More recently, AI systems have surpassed even the strongest human grandmasters by wide margins, and modern chess engines are now primarily used as training and analysis tools rather than opponents humans can realistically compete against.

Despite computers now vastly outperforming humans, competitive human chess has grown rather than diminished in popularity, particularly following a surge of interest linked to online chess platforms and popular media coverage, demonstrating that the game's appeal as a human intellectual and competitive pursuit remains strong regardless of computational dominance.

FAQ

What is the earliest known ancestor of chess?

Historians generally trace chess's origins to a 6th-century Indian game called chaturanga, whose name refers to the four divisions of an ancient Indian army, which evolved into the modern game's pieces over centuries.

How did chess get its modern rules?

Chess underwent significant rule changes in late 15th century Europe, when the queen and bishop pieces gained dramatically increased mobility, transforming the game's pace into something much closer to the modern version.

How do you win a game of chess?

The objective is to checkmate the opponent's king, meaning the king is under direct attack with no legal move to escape capture. Games can also end in a draw through stalemate, repetition, or the fifty-move rule.

Which chess piece is the most powerful?

The queen is the most powerful piece, able to move any number of squares in any straight or diagonal direction, combining the movement abilities of both the rook and bishop.

When did computers first beat the world chess champion?

IBM's Deep Blue defeated world champion Garry Kasparov in 1997, a widely covered milestone often cited as a turning point in public perception of computer intelligence.
