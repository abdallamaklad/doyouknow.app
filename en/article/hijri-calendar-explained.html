The Hijri Calendar Explained: How the Islamic Lunar Year Works

What Is the Hijri Calendar?

The Hijri calendar is the lunar calendar used by Muslims to determine religious dates. It begins with the Hijrah, the migration of the Prophet Muhammad (peace be upon him) from Mecca to Medina in 622 CE.

Unlike the Gregorian solar calendar, the Hijri calendar follows the moon. This means Islamic dates move backward through the seasons by about ten or eleven days each year.

The Lunar Year vs. the Solar Year

A lunar year has about 354 or 355 days, while a solar year has about 365.25 days. The difference of roughly eleven days causes Ramadan, Hajj, and Eid to shift through the seasons over a 33-year cycle.

This shifting has practical effects. Muslims may fast Ramadan in long summer days one year and short winter days a few years later. Pilgrims performing Hajj also experience different weather conditions.

The Twelve Months of the Hijri Calendar

The Islamic year has twelve months: Muharram, Safar, Rabi' al-Awwal, Rabi' al-Thani, Jumada al-Awwal, Jumada al-Thani, Rajab, Sha'ban, Ramadan, Shawwal, Dhul-Qa'dah, and Dhul-Hijjah.

Four of these months are considered sacred: Muharram, Rajab, Dhul-Qa'dah, and Dhul-Hijjah. Fighting was traditionally forbidden during them, and Muslims are encouraged to increase good deeds.

Moon Sighting or Astronomical Calculation?

Traditionally, each month begins when the new crescent moon is sighted after sunset. Today, many countries combine visual sighting with astronomical calculation.

Saudi Arabia and several Gulf states use the Umm al-Qura calendar, which is based on astronomical calculations, for civil planning. Other communities prefer physical moon sighting, which can lead to slight differences in start dates.

How the Hijri Calendar Shapes Worship

All major Islamic acts of worship are tied to the Hijri calendar. Ramadan falls in the ninth month. Hajj and Eid al-Adha occur in Dhul-Hijjah. Ashura fasting is observed on the tenth of Muharram.

Understanding the calendar helps Muslims prepare spiritually and logistically for these events. It also connects them to the historical Hijrah, which gave the calendar its name.

Converting Between Hijri and Gregorian Dates

There is no fixed one-to-one mapping between Hijri and Gregorian dates because the years are different lengths. Online converters and official calendars can estimate dates, but the exact start of a month depends on moon sighting or official announcement.

For important religious dates, it is best to check announcements from local religious authorities or the country you live in.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is the Islamic calendar called Hijri?

It begins from the year of the Hijrah, the migration of the Prophet Muhammad from Mecca to Medina in 622 CE.

How many days are in a Hijri year?

354 or 355 days, depending on whether the months are 29 or 30 days long.

Why do Ramadan and Eid move earlier each Gregorian year?

Because the Hijri lunar year is about eleven days shorter than the solar year.

Can Hijri dates be predicted in advance?

Astronomy can estimate dates, but the official start of a month often depends on moon sighting or national announcements.

Is the Hijri calendar used officially today?

Yes, many Muslim-majority countries use it alongside the Gregorian calendar for religious and sometimes civil purposes.
