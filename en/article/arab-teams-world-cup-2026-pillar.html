Arab Football at World Cup 2026

The 2026 FIFA World Cup in North America represents a historic moment for Arab football. With eight teams from the Arab world qualifying, this is the largest representation the region has ever sent to a single tournament. The expansion to 48 teams created additional slots, but Arab teams earned their places through genuine competitive achievement. Morocco's stunning semi-final run in 2022 changed global perceptions of what Arab and African football could achieve. The question now is whether 2026 will cement that legacy or prove it was an exception.

The eight Arab qualifiers span North Africa, the Gulf, and the Levant. They bring diverse tactical approaches, distinct football cultures, and varying levels of preparation. Morocco and Tunisia arrive as established continental powers with World Cup experience. Saudi Arabia and Qatar carry the weight of Gulf investment and ambition. Algeria brings a blend of European-based talent and African physicality. Egypt represents the most populous Arab nation and its most passionate football culture. Iraq and Jordan are the surprises, proving that underdog narratives still matter in Arab football.

This guide covers every Arab team at World Cup 2026, providing squad profiles, tactical analysis, historical context, and realistic expectations. For detailed analysis of each team, follow the links to our dedicated articles.

Morocco: The Atlas Lions

Morocco enters the 2026 World Cup as Africa's highest-ranked team and one of the tournament's most compelling stories. The semi-final run in Qatar 2022 was not merely a fluke. It was the culmination of a systematic development program that leveraged Morocco's dual football heritage: European tactical sophistication combined with African athleticism and intensity.

Head coach Walid Regragui has built a cohesive unit that prioritizes defensive organization without sacrificing attacking threat. The backline, anchored by Achraf Hakimi, Noussair Mazraoui, and Nayef Aguerd, is among the most experienced in the tournament. Sofyan Amrabat's midfield destruction became legendary in 2022, and his continued presence provides the tactical foundation around which the team operates.

The attacking evolution is the key question. Youssef En-Nesyri provides aerial threat and hold-up play, but Morocco's reliance on set pieces and counter-attacks must expand if they are to replicate their 2022 success. The emergence of young talents from the diaspora and the domestic league has deepened the squad. Morocco's group draw will be critical, but they have demonstrated that they can trouble any opponent on any continent.

Saudi Arabia: Green Falcons

Saudi Arabia's national team carries the weight of a nation's sporting ambition. The stunning victory over Argentina in the 2022 World Cup opening match remains one of the greatest upsets in tournament history. The subsequent domestic league revolution, bankrolled by the Public Investment Fund, has transformed the football landscape. Cristiano Ronaldo, Karim Benzema, Neymar, and dozens of other global stars now play in the Saudi Pro League, creating unprecedented exposure for Saudi football.

The challenge is converting domestic league investment into national team performance. Head coach Roberto Mancini, appointed in 2023, has brought European tactical sophistication to a squad that historically relied on physical fitness and collective organization. Salem Al-Dawsari remains the creative heartbeat, while the emergence of younger talents from the league's new competitive environment has strengthened the depth.

The Saudi Pro League's internationalization creates a paradox. The league is stronger than ever, but the national team must compete for playing time against global superstars. The 2026 World Cup will test whether the Green Falcons can harness the energy of their domestic transformation into a coherent international force. The opening match against Argentina proved they can beat anyone on a single day. The question is whether they can sustain that level across a tournament.

Tunisia: Carthage Eagles

Tunisia represents the consistent, disciplined tradition of North African football. The Carthage Eagles have qualified for multiple consecutive World Cups, and their 2022 performance, particularly the victory against France in the final group match, demonstrated their capability. They are not flashy, but they are organized, experienced, and tactically intelligent.

The squad is built around a core of European-based professionals who understand tournament football. Wahbi Khazri has retired, but the midfield duo of Ellyes Skhiri and Aissa Laidouni provides a solid foundation. The defense is compact, and the team's pressing structure is among the most systematic in African football. Head coach Jalel Kadri has maintained continuity while integrating younger players.

Tunisia's limitation is clear: they lack the individual brilliance of Morocco or the financial resources of Saudi Arabia and Qatar. Their strength is collective execution. They will not dominate possession against elite opponents, but they will make every match difficult. In a 48-team tournament where the margins between advancement and elimination are thin, Tunisia's reliability is an asset.

Algeria: Desert Warriors

Algeria's 2026 qualification is a redemption story. The 2019 Africa Cup of Nations champions missed the 2022 World Cup entirely, a humiliation that triggered significant soul-searching within the federation. The response has been emphatic: a rebuilt squad, a renewed tactical approach, and the return of key players from the diaspora.

Riyad Mahrez remains the symbolic leader, though his role has evolved from the creative focal point to an experienced mentor. The emergence of younger talents in European leagues has created a more dynamic squad. Ismael Bennacer, if fit, provides midfield control that Algeria has often lacked in major tournaments. The defense has been rebuilt with a focus on pace and organization.

Head coach Djamel Belmadi has restored the tactical discipline that defined the 2019 AFCON victory. Algeria's 4-3-3 system emphasizes high pressing and quick transitions, a style that can trouble teams that prefer controlled possession. The question is whether the squad can maintain intensity through a long tournament. The 2022 disappointment was partly attributed to complacency; the 2026 squad appears hungrier.

Egypt: Pharaohs

Egypt carries the weight of the most populous Arab nation and its most passionate football culture. The Pharaohs have qualified for the World Cup only three times in their history, and their 2018 appearance ended in group-stage elimination. The 2026 qualification is a statement of intent from a nation that believes it deserves a permanent place on the global stage.

Mohamed Salah remains the most recognizable Arab footballer in the world. His continued brilliance at Liverpool provides the attacking foundation around which the entire team is built. The supporting cast has evolved. Trezeguet, Omar Marmoush, and Mohamed Abdelmonem provide additional threat, while the midfield has been strengthened by the emergence of European-based players.

The tactical challenge is creating a system that maximizes Salah without becoming overly dependent on him. Head coach Hossam Hassan has experimented with formations that allow Salah to operate from various positions while maintaining defensive solidity. Egypt's defense, historically a weakness, has improved with the integration of younger players and the experience of Ahmed Hegazi.

The psychological pressure on Egypt is immense. A nation of 105 million people expects progression from the group stage. The question is whether the squad can convert domestic passion into international performance. Egypt's 2026 journey will be one of the most emotionally charged stories of the tournament.

Qatar: Maroons

Qatar's presence at the 2026 World Cup is proof that the 2022 hosting experience was not a one-off indulgence. The national team that won the 2019 Asian Cup and competed on home soil in 2022 has evolved. The Aspire Academy production line continues to generate technically gifted players, and the domestic league's professionalization has created a deeper competitive environment.

Head coach Tintin Marquez has maintained the possession-based philosophy that defines Qatari football. Akram Afif and Almoez Ali remain the attacking core, and both have matured significantly since 2022. Afif's dribbling ability and Ali's finishing make them one of the most dangerous attacking duos in Asian football. The midfield, built around the technically excellent Homam Ahmed and Abdullah Al-Ahrak, prioritizes ball retention and progressive passing.

The challenge for Qatar is physical. In 2022, they were overwhelmed by the intensity of Ecuador and Senegal. The 2026 squad is more physically prepared, but the question remains whether they can compete with the athleticism of African and European opponents. The expanded format means they are likely to face a more diverse range of opponents, and their technical quality may be decisive against less organized teams.

Iraq: Lions of Mesopotamia

Iraq's qualification for the 2026 World Cup is one of the great stories of the cycle. A nation that has endured decades of conflict, sanctions, and institutional instability has produced a football team that embodies resilience. The Lions of Mesopotamia have always been more than a sports team; they have been a symbol of national unity in a divided country.

Head coach Jesus Casas has built a squad that combines domestic league players with a growing diaspora contingent. The tactical approach is pragmatic, built on defensive solidity and rapid transitions. Aymen Hussein has emerged as the attacking focal point, while the defense, marshaled by experienced professionals, is organized and determined.

Iraq's qualification itself is the achievement. Expectations for the tournament should be modest, but the experience of participating in a World Cup on foreign soil, after decades of exclusion, is culturally significant. The 2007 Asian Cup victory proved that Iraq can achieve the extraordinary when unity prevails. The 2026 squad carries that legacy.

Jordan: Nashama

Jordan's qualification for the 2026 World Cup represents the culmination of a long-term development project. The Jordan Football Association has invested systematically in youth development, and the results are now visible at the senior level. The Nashama have qualified for their first World Cup, a historic achievement for a nation of just 11 million people.

The squad is built around a core of professionals in the Middle Eastern leagues and a growing European contingent. Musa Al-Taamari is the standout talent, a winger with pace and creativity who has attracted attention from European clubs. The defense, organized by coach Hussein Ammouta, is disciplined and difficult to break down. Jordan's approach is similar to Tunisia's: collective organization over individual brilliance.

The World Cup experience itself will be invaluable for Jordan's football development. The exposure to elite opposition, the organizational experience of tournament football, and the inspiration for the next generation of young players are the true prizes. A single victory or even a competitive draw would exceed most expectations and cement Jordan's place in football history.

Historical Context: Arab Teams at Past World Cups

The history of Arab teams at the World Cup is a story of gradual progress punctuated by moments of breakthrough.

Egypt was the first Arab nation to participate, in 1934, but did not return until 1990. The long gaps between appearances reflect the historical challenges facing football development in the region. Tunisia's first appearance in 1978 produced the first African team to win a World Cup match, defeating Mexico 3-1. Algeria's 1982 team was controversially eliminated after West Germany and Austria's infamous collusion, a trauma that still shapes Algerian football culture.

Saudi Arabia's 1994 team achieved the breakthrough that established the modern template for Gulf football, reaching the Round of 16 with memorable victories over Belgium and Morocco. The subsequent decline was equally dramatic, with heavy defeats in 1998 and 2002. Morocco's 1986 team was the first African side to reach the Round of 16, a milestone that stood until Senegal replicated it in 2002.

The 2022 Qatar World Cup changed everything. Morocco's semi-final run was not merely the greatest Arab achievement; it was the greatest African achievement and one of the most significant underdog stories in modern football history. Saudi Arabia's defeat of Argentina proved that even the greatest teams could be vulnerable. Tunisia's victory over France, even in a dead rubber, demonstrated the narrowing gap between Arab teams and European elite.

The 2026 tournament represents a new era. Eight teams, expanded opportunity, and a generation of players who have grown up believing that the semi-final is possible, not merely the group stage. The historical context is important, but the present moment is unprecedented.

What to Expect in 2026

The 48-team format fundamentally changes the tournament dynamics. The group stage is more forgiving, with three teams advancing from each group. This increases the likelihood that Arab teams will progress beyond the opening round, but it also means that the Round of 32 will feature more variable opposition.

The geographic spread of the tournament across the United States, Canada, and Mexico creates travel challenges that will test squad depth and logistics. European-based players will be familiar with transatlantic travel, but domestic league players from Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and Iraq may face adjustment challenges. The summer timing, rather than the winter scheduling of Qatar 2022, returns to traditional football rhythms.

Realistic expectations vary by team. Morocco and Saudi Arabia have the talent and experience to reach the Round of 16 and potentially beyond. Tunisia, Algeria, and Egypt should target group-stage progression. Qatar, Iraq, and Jordan are primarily seeking competitive performances and the developmental experience of tournament football. But the 2022 World Cup proved that expectations are often irrelevant in a single-match knockout format.

The collective performance matters. If multiple Arab teams progress beyond the group stage, it will represent a regional statement comparable to the African advances of the 1990s and 2000s. The 2026 World Cup is an opportunity for Arab football to demonstrate that Qatar 2022 was the beginning of a new era, not an isolated moment.

FAQ

How many Arab teams qualified for World Cup 2026?

Eight Arab teams qualified for the 2026 FIFA World Cup: Morocco, Saudi Arabia, Tunisia, Algeria, Egypt, Qatar, Iraq, and Jordan. This is the largest Arab representation in World Cup history, made possible by both the tournament's expansion to 48 teams and genuine competitive achievement by the qualifying nations.

Which Arab team has the best chance to win World Cup 2026?

Morocco is the highest-ranked Arab team and enters the tournament with the strongest recent World Cup record, having reached the semi-finals in 2022. Saudi Arabia and Tunisia also have realistic ambitions of progressing beyond the group stage. No Arab team is among the tournament favorites, but Morocco has demonstrated that deep runs are possible.

Did Egypt qualify for World Cup 2026?

Yes, Egypt qualified for the 2026 World Cup. The Pharaohs, led by Mohamed Salah, secured their place through the African qualification process. This is their fourth World Cup appearance and their first since 2018. Egypt carries significant expectations from the most populous Arab nation.

Where is World Cup 2026 being held?

The 2026 FIFA World Cup is being hosted by the United States, Canada, and Mexico. It is the first World Cup with three host nations and the first to feature 48 teams. The tournament will be played across 16 cities in North America, with the final scheduled for July 19, 2026, at MetLife Stadium in New York/New Jersey.

What time of year is World Cup 2026?

The 2026 World Cup will be held from June 11 to July 19, 2026. This is a traditional summer scheduling, unlike the winter timing of Qatar 2022. The North American summer will present heat challenges in some southern venues, but the tournament schedule and stadium infrastructure are designed to manage these conditions.
