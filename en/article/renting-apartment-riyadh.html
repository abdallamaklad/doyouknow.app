Renting an Apartment in Riyadh: Contracts, Ejar, and Costs

doyouknow.app Editorial Team2026-07-1014 min read

Editorial note: This guide was checked against official Saudi government sources and service portals on 2026-07-10. Fees, procedures, and eligibility rules can change, so confirm details through the official channels linked below before acting.

Renting in Riyadh: An Overview

Riyadh is the political, commercial, and cultural center of Saudi Arabia. Every year, thousands of expatriates and Saudis move to the capital for work, study, or family reasons. Finding the right apartment can be exciting but also challenging, especially for newcomers unfamiliar with local rental laws and market practices.

The rental market in Riyadh offers a wide range of options, from small studio apartments in older neighborhoods to luxury compounds and high-rise towers in the north of the city. Prices vary significantly based on location, building age, amenities, and whether the unit is furnished or unfurnished. Understanding the market helps you set a realistic budget and avoid scams.

This guide explains the entire rental process step by step. We cover how to search for apartments, what to look for during viewings, how to read and register an Ejar contract, and what rights and responsibilities renters have under Saudi law.

Popular Neighborhoods and Price Ranges

North Riyadh neighborhoods such as Al Yasmin, Al Nakheel, Al Aqiq, and Al Mohammadiyah are popular among expatriates and affluent families. These areas offer modern buildings, parks, shopping centers, and international schools. Rents here are among the highest in the city, with two-bedroom apartments often ranging from SAR 60,000 to SAR 120,000 per year depending on quality and exact location.

Central and older districts such as Al Batha, Al Dirah, and Al Malaz are more affordable. These areas have older buildings but better access to public services, souks, and public transportation. A two-bedroom apartment in these neighborhoods may cost between SAR 25,000 and SAR 50,000 per year.

Eastern Riyadh neighborhoods such as Al Rawdah and Al Khaleej are convenient for families who want space at a moderate price. Western areas near the airport are popular with airline staff and logistics workers. Before choosing, consider your commute, school needs, parking availability, and proximity to hospitals and supermarkets.

How to Search for an Apartment

Most renters in Riyadh start their search online. Popular property portals list apartments with photos, prices, and contact details. You can filter by neighborhood, number of bedrooms, price range, and amenities. Social media groups and classified websites also list rentals, but these require extra caution because scams are more common.

Real estate offices can help if you want a professional search. Agents usually charge a commission equal to a percentage of the annual rent, often around 2.5% to 5%, or a fixed fee. Make sure the agent is licensed by the General Authority for Real Estate. Ask for a written agreement before paying any commission.

Word of mouth remains valuable. Asking colleagues, friends, or community members about available apartments can lead to better deals and trustworthy landlords. When you find a promising listing, arrange a viewing as soon as possible, because good apartments in popular areas rent quickly.

What to Check During a Viewing

During the viewing, check the apartment thoroughly. Test taps, showers, toilets, lights, air conditioners, and kitchen appliances. Ask about the age of the building and whether major maintenance is planned. Look for signs of water damage, cracks, or pest problems.

Check the parking situation. Many apartments include one parking space, but some do not. If you have more than one car, confirm whether extra parking is available. Ask about the building’s security, elevator maintenance, and whether there is a guard or reception desk.

Find out what is included in the rent. Some landlords include water and electricity up to a certain amount, while others expect tenants to pay all utilities directly. Internet may or may not be available in the building. Clarify these details before signing anything.

The Ejar Contract and Registration

In Saudi Arabia, rental contracts should be registered through the Ejar platform operated by the Ministry of Housing. The Ejar contract protects both landlord and tenant by creating an official record of the agreement. It is especially important for expatriates because a registered rental contract is often required for iqama address updates and family visa applications.

The contract must include the names of the landlord and tenant, the property address, the annual rent, the payment schedule, the lease duration, and any special conditions. Read every clause carefully. Pay attention to renewal terms, rent increase limits, maintenance responsibilities, and early termination penalties.

Both parties sign the Ejar contract electronically. Once registered, you can download a copy from the Ejar website or app. Keep this document safe. If a dispute arises, the Ejar record is the official reference used by housing committees and courts.

Rental Costs and Deposits

Rents in Riyadh are usually quoted as annual amounts, but payment terms vary. Some landlords require the full year upfront, while others accept two, four, or six payments. Negotiating the payment schedule can make a big difference to your cash flow. Make sure the schedule is written in the contract.

A security deposit is standard practice. It is usually one or two months’ rent and is refundable at the end of the lease, minus any deductions for damage beyond normal wear and tear. Get a written receipt for the deposit and clarify the conditions for refund.

In addition to rent, budget for utilities, internet, furniture, and moving costs. Some compounds and luxury buildings charge monthly service fees for maintenance, security, and amenities. Ask whether these fees are included in the rent or paid separately.

Renter Rights and Responsibilities

Saudi rental law gives tenants important protections. The landlord cannot evict you without legal cause and proper notice. Rent increases are capped and must follow the rules set by the Ministry of Housing. The landlord is generally responsible for major repairs, while the tenant handles minor maintenance and keeps the property clean.

Tenants must pay rent on time and follow the terms of the contract. Subletting without the landlord’s written consent is usually prohibited. Making major alterations, such as painting walls or installing fixtures, often requires permission. At the end of the lease, the tenant must return the apartment in the same condition, allowing for normal wear and tear.

If a dispute occurs, tenants can file a complaint through the Ejar platform or the relevant housing committee. Bring your registered contract, payment receipts, and any correspondence with the landlord. Keeping good records is the best way to protect yourself.

Moving In and Final Tips

Before moving in, take photos or a video of the entire apartment, including any existing damage. Share these with the landlord so there is no disagreement later. Test all utilities and make sure electricity, water, and internet accounts are set up in your name or included in the rent.

Register your address with the relevant authorities if required. A registered Ejar contract is usually accepted as proof of address. Update your address in Absher so that official correspondence reaches you.

Build a good relationship with your landlord or property manager. Prompt communication helps resolve maintenance issues quickly. By understanding the market, reading the contract carefully, and keeping records, you can enjoy a smooth renting experience in Riyadh.

Furnished vs Unfurnished Apartments

Furnished apartments are convenient for short-term stays or newcomers who do not own furniture. They usually include appliances, sofas, beds, and sometimes linens and kitchenware. The monthly or annual rent is higher than an unfurnished unit, but you avoid the upfront cost and hassle of buying furniture.

Unfurnished apartments give you the freedom to decorate and choose your own furniture. They are usually cheaper and may be larger. However, you must budget for beds, sofas, refrigerators, washing machines, and curtains. If you plan to stay for several years, unfurnished is often more economical.

When viewing a furnished apartment, check the condition of the furniture and appliances. Ask whether maintenance is included. Some furnished units are managed by companies that replace worn items, while others leave everything to the tenant.

Compound Living and Gated Communities

Compounds are residential communities popular among expatriates, especially families. They usually offer villas or apartments within a gated area with shared facilities such as swimming pools, gyms, playgrounds, and sometimes shops and schools. Security is typically higher than in regular neighborhoods.

Rent in compounds is higher than comparable apartments outside, but many residents value the lifestyle and community. Some compounds have waiting lists, so apply early. Contracts may include rules about guests, pets, and noise.

Before choosing a compound, visit at different times of day. Check the condition of shared facilities and ask about service charges. Talk to current residents if possible to learn about management quality and community atmosphere.

Negotiating Rent and Payment Terms

Rent prices in Riyadh are often negotiable, especially if you are willing to sign a longer lease or pay several months in advance. Politely ask the landlord if there is flexibility in the price. Pointing out minor issues, such as outdated paint or old appliances, can sometimes lead to a discount.

Payment terms are another area for negotiation. If the landlord asks for the full year upfront, you can propose four or six checks instead. Some landlords accept this if it means securing a reliable tenant. Always put the agreed terms in writing as part of the Ejar contract.

Do not agree to cash payments without a receipt. All rent payments should be documented through bank transfer or checks. This protects both parties if a dispute arises.

Maintenance Responsibilities Explained

Maintenance responsibilities should be clearly stated in the Ejar contract. Generally, the landlord is responsible for structural repairs, plumbing, electrical systems, air conditioning, and major appliances if they are included in the rental. The tenant is responsible for minor repairs, changing light bulbs, and keeping the property clean.

If something breaks, notify the landlord or property manager in writing. Keep a record of the request and any responses. For urgent issues such as water leaks or electrical faults, the landlord should respond quickly to prevent further damage.

Some landlords include a maintenance clause that requires tenants to pay for repairs below a certain amount. Read this clause carefully. Taking photos before and after repairs helps avoid disputes when moving out.

Moving Out and Getting Your Deposit Back

When your lease ends, give the landlord written notice according to the contract terms, usually 30 to 90 days in advance. Schedule a final inspection with the landlord or agent. Clean the apartment thoroughly and repair any damage beyond normal wear and tear.

During the inspection, compare the current condition with the move-in photos. If everything is in order, the landlord should return your deposit. Some landlords deduct cleaning fees or repair costs. These deductions should be reasonable and documented.

If the landlord refuses to return the deposit without justification, you can file a complaint through the Ejar platform or the housing committee. Bring your registered contract, receipts, and photos. Following the correct process improves your chances of a fair outcome.

Utilities and Connection Fees

When moving into a new apartment, you may need to set up electricity, water, internet, and gas services. The Saudi Electricity Company and National Water Company supply most properties. You can open accounts online or through their apps using your iqama and rental contract.

Some landlords keep utility accounts in their name and include a fixed amount in the rent. Others require tenants to open accounts themselves. Ask who is responsible before signing the lease. Connection or transfer fees may apply, especially for new buildings.

Internet providers such as STC, Mobily, and Zain offer home broadband packages. Installation usually takes a few days. Compare speeds, data caps, and contract lengths. Some buildings have exclusive agreements with one provider, limiting your choices.

Dealing with Rent Increases

Saudi rental law limits how much a landlord can increase rent when renewing a lease. The exact percentage may change, so check the latest rules from the Ministry of Municipalities and Housing. Any increase must be stated in the renewal contract and agreed upon by both parties.

If you receive a renewal offer with a large increase, compare it to similar apartments in the area. Use this information to negotiate. If the landlord insists on an increase above the legal limit, you can file a complaint through the Ejar platform or the housing committee.

Keep copies of previous contracts to show your rental history. A good tenant who pays on time and maintains the property has strong negotiating power. Being informed about your rights prevents unfair increases.

Renting with Pets

Pet policies vary widely in Riyadh. Many apartment buildings do not allow pets, while some compounds and villas are more flexible. If you have a dog, cat, or bird, ask about pet rules before signing a lease. Some landlords charge a pet deposit or require a veterinary certificate.

Cats and small birds are generally more accepted than large dogs. Some compounds have green areas where dogs can be walked. Keep your pet clean and quiet to avoid complaints from neighbors. Respecting building rules protects your tenancy.

If you cannot find a pet-friendly apartment, consider asking your employer for housing advice or searching in villa compounds. Starting your search early gives you more options. Never hide a pet from a landlord, as this can lead to lease termination.

Short-Term Rentals and Hotels

If you need a place for a few weeks while searching for a long-term apartment, short-term rentals and serviced apartments are available. These are furnished and include utilities, internet, and cleaning. Prices are higher than long-term rent but offer flexibility.

Hotels and serviced apartments are regulated and can be booked through international platforms. Make sure the property allows the length of stay you need and that the neighborhood suits your commute. Some employers provide temporary housing for new employees.

Short-term rentals should not be used as a permanent address for iqama registration. Once you find a long-term apartment, register the Ejar contract and update your address in Absher.

Riyadh Rental Market Trends

Riyadh’s rental market is growing as the city expands under Vision 2030. New neighborhoods, entertainment districts, and business hubs are attracting residents. Demand for quality apartments is high, especially in northern and western areas close to new developments.

Rents have been rising in popular districts, but older central areas still offer value. The market also sees seasonal fluctuations, with more listings available after major holidays. Monitoring listings for several weeks helps you understand fair prices.

Future infrastructure projects, such as the Riyadh Metro, may change which neighborhoods are desirable. Areas near new metro stations may become more convenient and more expensive. Considering future development helps you choose a home with long-term value.

Frequently asked questions

How much does it cost to rent a two-bedroom apartment in Riyadh?

It ranges from about SAR 25,000 in older central areas to over SAR 120,000 in premium northern neighborhoods.

What is Ejar?

Ejar is the Saudi Ministry of Housing platform for registering rental contracts electronically.

Is a registered rental contract required?

It is strongly recommended and often needed for iqama address updates and family visa applications.

How are rents usually paid?

Many landlords require the full year upfront, but some accept multiple post-dated checks.

What should I check before signing a lease?

Verify the property condition, payment schedule, deposit terms, maintenance responsibilities, and contract renewal rules.

Can a landlord increase rent at any time?

No. Rent increases are regulated and must follow Ministry of Housing rules and the contract terms.

What if I have a dispute with my landlord?

File a complaint through the Ejar platform or the housing committee, and bring your registered contract and receipts.
