What Is a Healthy Resting Heart Rate by Age and Fitness

Introduction

Your resting heart rate — the number of times your heart beats per minute while you sit quietly — is one of the simplest vital signs you can measure yourself, and one of the most informative. It reflects how efficiently your heart pumps, how fit you are, and sometimes whether something in your body needs attention.

For most adults, a resting rate between 60 and 100 beats per minute is considered normal. But within that range, lower is generally better: large studies link resting rates in the 50s and 60s with lower cardiovascular risk, while rates persistently above 80 are associated with higher risk, even in people who feel fine.

What Counts as Normal by Age

Normal resting rates shift across life. Newborns race along at 100 to 160 beats per minute, and the rate falls steadily through childhood: roughly 90 to 140 for toddlers, 75 to 115 for school-age children, and 60 to 100 by the teenage years, where it stays through adulthood. Older adults remain in the 60 to 100 range, though the heart's maximum rate declines with age.

Within the adult range, position in the range matters. A resting rate consistently near 100, even though technically normal, is associated in cohort studies with higher long-term cardiovascular risk than a rate in the 60s. Doctors therefore treat the 60s and low 70s as the comfortable zone for most healthy adults.

What Fitness Does to Heart Rate

Endurance training physically remodels the heart: the left ventricle grows stronger and more elastic, and each beat ejects more blood. Because the heart delivers the same blood flow with fewer beats, resting rate falls. Well-trained recreational athletes commonly rest in the 50s, and elite endurance athletes can drop into the 30s and 40s — cyclist Miguel Indurain was famously reported at 28 beats per minute.

This is why a low resting heart rate is usually a badge of fitness rather than a problem. The caveat: a slow rate in an untrained person, especially with dizziness, fatigue, or fainting, can signal a conduction problem in the heart's electrical system rather than athletic conditioning. Context — how you feel and how you train — decides which story the number tells.

How to Measure Your Resting Heart Rate

Measure it at a true rest: ideally in the morning before getting out of bed, or after sitting quietly for at least five minutes. Press two fingers — not the thumb, which has its own pulse — to the wrist below the base of the thumb or to the side of the neck, count beats for 30 seconds, and double the number. Counting for a full minute is more accurate if your rhythm is irregular.

Take readings on several days rather than trusting one number, because caffeine, poor sleep, stress, dehydration, fever, and some medications all push the rate up temporarily. Wearables do a decent job of tracking resting trends automatically, but an occasional manual check is a good calibration. What matters most is your personal baseline and whether it drifts.

When the Number Deserves Attention

A resting rate consistently above 100 is called tachycardia, and one consistently below 60 bradycardia — neither is automatically dangerous, but both warrant a conversation with a clinician, especially with symptoms. A sudden unexplained jump of 10 to 15 beats above your baseline, sustained for days, can signal infection, anemia, thyroid problems, dehydration, or overtraining.

Seek urgent care if an abnormal rate comes with chest pain, severe shortness of breath, fainting, or a racing, irregular heartbeat. For everyone else, the resting heart rate is best used as a gentle trend tracker: as fitness improves it drifts down, and when life gets stressful or sleep suffers, it quietly drifts up.

FAQ

What is a normal resting heart rate for adults?

For most adults, 60 to 100 beats per minute is the normal resting range. Within that range, lower tends to be healthier — rates in the 50s and 60s are associated with lower cardiovascular risk, while rates persistently above 80 carry higher risk even in people who feel well.

Is a resting heart rate of 50 too low?

Usually not, if you are physically active. Endurance training strengthens the heart so it pumps more blood per beat, and fit people commonly rest in the 50s or even 40s. But a rate that low in an untrained person, especially with dizziness or fatigue, should be checked by a doctor.

How do I check my resting heart rate?

Rest quietly for five minutes — ideally measure first thing in the morning. Place two fingers on your wrist below the thumb or on the side of your neck, count beats for 30 seconds, and double the result. Repeat on several days to find your baseline, since caffeine, stress, and poor sleep temporarily raise it.

Why is my resting heart rate higher than usual?

Common temporary causes include caffeine, stress, poor sleep, dehydration, fever, and some medications. A sustained rise of 10 to 15 beats above your normal baseline can also signal infection, anemia, thyroid issues, or overtraining, and is worth discussing with a clinician if it persists.

Can exercise lower my resting heart rate?

Yes. Regular aerobic exercise strengthens the heart muscle so each beat moves more blood, and the heart needs fewer beats at rest. Consistent endurance training over months typically lowers resting heart rate by several beats per minute — one of the clearest signs of improving cardiovascular fitness.
