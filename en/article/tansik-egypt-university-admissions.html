Tansik: Egypt's University Admissions System Explained

Introduction

Every summer, hundreds of thousands of Egyptian families follow the same ritual: tracking Tansik (التنسيق), the centralized electronic coordination system that decides which public university or institute a student will join. Run under the Ministry of Higher Education, Tansik is the single official gateway for admission to Egypt's public universities for holders of the Thanaweya Amma (general secondary) certificate and several other qualifications.

This guide explains how the system works: what Tansik actually is, how its admission stages are organized, how minimum scores are set each year, and how students register their preferences step by step on the official portal.

What Is Tansik?

Tansik is Egypt's centralized university admissions office. Instead of applying to each university separately, a student submits one ranked list of preferences — faculties and higher institutes — through a single national portal, and the system matches students to available seats based on their total secondary-school score, the order of their preferences, and published capacity for each program.

The service moved online in the early 2000s and is now accessed through the official Tansik portal (tansik.egypt.gov.eg), which handles general secondary students, technical diploma holders, and holders of equivalent Arab and foreign certificates in separate tracks. Egypt has more than 25 public universities, and admission to their regular, state-funded programs runs almost entirely through this system, while private and national (non-profit) universities use their own platforms.

How the Coordination Stages Work

Admissions proceed in consecutive stages. Stage 1 opens for the highest scorers in each Thanaweya Amma branch — science, mathematics, and literature — with a published minimum threshold for joining the stage. Once stage 1 results are announced, stage 2 opens for the next band of scores, followed by stage 3, which typically covers the remaining students and those who passed second-round resit exams. Technical diploma holders are coordinated through their own announced stages.

Two rules shape every placement. The first is the score: each faculty's cut-off is effectively set by supply and demand, so the total score of the last admitted student becomes the published minimum for that year. The second is geographic distribution: for many faculties, priority goes to students from the surrounding region, while leading faculties such as medicine and engineering admit students from across the country.

Minimum Scores and Cut-offs

Cut-offs change every year and become final only when a stage closes, so any specific number should be treated as indicative rather than guaranteed. In recent admission cycles, the entry thresholds for stage 1 have typically fallen in the high-80s to low-90s percent range for the science and mathematics branches and around the high-60s for literature, while stage-2 thresholds have ranged roughly between the mid-50s and high-60s depending on the branch and the year.

At the faculty level, recent cycles have placed medicine cut-offs in the low-to-mid 90s percent, dentistry and pharmacy around 91–93%, engineering in the mid-to-high 80s, and leading literature-track faculties such as economics and political science, Alsun (languages), and mass communication roughly between the mid-80s and 90%. The Ministry of Higher Education announces the official figures after each stage's results are published.

How to Apply Step by Step

First, wait for your stage: after Thanaweya Amma results are announced, the ministry publishes the dates and entry thresholds for each stage, and you join when your score band opens. Then open the official Tansik portal and sign in with your seat number and the secret number printed on your results statement — keep both private, since anyone holding them can edit your choices.

Next, order your preferences carefully. The system reads your list from top to bottom, so place your most-wanted faculty first while respecting the geographic distribution rules, and use the full list length available to you. Save the form and print it; you can return and edit the list at any time before your stage closes, and only the last saved version counts.

When results appear on the same portal, print the nomination card and submit your documents to the nominated faculty before its deadline. A separate online window for transfers and for "reducing alienation" (taqlil al-ightirab) usually opens after the first two stages for students who want a closer or more suitable faculty.

One more step applies to some students: faculties such as fine arts, applied arts, physical education, and music education require passing a practical aptitude test before they can even be listed. Registration for these tests opens on the Tansik portal before the coordination stages, and recent cycles charged a fee of roughly 800 Egyptian pounds per test.

FAQ

Who must apply through Tansik?

Any student seeking admission to an Egyptian public university or government higher institute on the basis of the Thanaweya Amma certificate. Technical diploma holders and holders of equivalent Arab or foreign certificates have their own coordination tracks on the same portal, while private universities run separate admissions.

What do I need to register on the Tansik portal?

You need your seat number and the secret number printed on your results statement. Keep both private, review your personal data shown on the portal for accuracy, and print your preferences form after saving it.

How are the minimum scores decided each year?

By supply and demand within each stage. The ministry publishes entry thresholds for every stage, and each faculty's final cut-off equals the total score of the last student admitted to it, which is why cut-offs move from year to year and are only known after a stage closes.

Can I change my preferences after submitting them?

Yes, as long as your stage is still open: you can sign in again and modify your list, and the system only considers the last saved version. After the stage closes, changes are possible only through the official transfer and reducing-alienation windows.

What are the aptitude tests and who needs them?

They are practical exams required by certain talent-based faculties, such as fine arts, applied arts, physical education, and music education. Students must register for these tests on the Tansik portal before the coordination stages and pass them to be allowed to list those faculties among their preferences.
