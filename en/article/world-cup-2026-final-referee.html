Why the Final Referee Matters

No official in world football works under more pressure than the World Cup final referee. A single whistle can decide the biggest match in sport, in front of a television audience that runs into the hundreds of millions. That is why the question "who will referee the final?" trends in Saudi Arabia and across the Arab world every time a World Cup reaches its closing days: fans want to know whose hands the trophy might pass through before a ball is even kicked.

The answer is not a lottery and not a political appointment in the simple sense. FIFA runs a structured, multi-year selection system that grades referees long before the tournament begins and keeps grading them after every match they handle. This guide explains how that system works: who makes the decision, which criteria matter, why neutrality rules narrow the field, who has refereed past finals, and what is known about the process for the 2026 final in North America.

The Referees Committee and the Selection Process

Responsibility for match officials sits with the FIFA Referees Committee, one of FIFA's standing committees, working alongside the FIFA Refereeing department. Pierluigi Collina — himself the referee of the 2002 final — has led FIFA's refereeing structure since 2017, first as chairman of the Referees Committee and later as head of refereeing. This group draws up the list of officials for each World Cup, monitors them through the tournament, and proposes the appointments for the showpiece matches.

The pipeline starts years before kickoff. Candidates must already hold a place on the FIFA International Refereeing List, which their national association renews annually. From that pool, FIFA builds a prospective World Cup panel and puts it through seminars, fitness testing, laws-of-the-game assessments, and trial assignments at youth World Cups, continental championships, and the Club World Cup. For the 2026 tournament, the panel spans all six confederations, reflecting the expanded 48-team field.

Selection does not stop when the tournament starts. Every performance is reviewed by analysts and former elite referees: key decisions, positioning, game management, and cooperation with the video team are all graded. Officials with the strongest marks are kept for the knockout rounds, and the names for the semi-finals and final are typically confirmed only two to four days before each match — once the teams involved are known and neutrality checks can be applied.

The Criteria: Form, Fitness, and Neutrality

Current form carries the most weight. FIFA's reviewers look closely at the accuracy of big calls — penalties, red cards, offside decisions in scoring situations — and at how calmly a referee controls high-stakes matches. A referee who has had a quiet, error-free tournament is a stronger candidate than a more famous name coming off a controversial game.

Fitness is the second filter. International referees pass repeated sprint and endurance tests, and the job demands they keep up with counter-attacks played at full speed. FIFA historically applied an age ceiling of around 45 to its international list, although exemptions for elite officials have softened that line in recent cycles. Consistency with the video assistant referee system is a third filter: the modern final referee must run a smooth workflow with the VAR room, including on-field reviews and, in recent FIFA competitions, announcing reviewed decisions to the stadium.

Then comes neutrality. A referee cannot come from the same country as either finalist, and FIFA generally avoids officials from the same confederation as a finalist when a credible neutral alternative exists. Officials who have already refereed one of the finalists in the tournament can still be considered, but the committee weighs familiarity carefully. Finally, the referee is appointed together with their regular team: two assistant referees, a fourth official, and a VAR crew they have worked with throughout the competition.

Past Finals: From Collina to Marciniak

The list of recent final referees reads like a hall of fame. Pierluigi Collina of Italy handled the 2002 final between Brazil and Germany in Yokohama. Horacio Elizondo of Argentina took charge in 2006, England's Howard Webb refereed the bruising 2010 final between the Netherlands and Spain, and Nicola Rizzoli of Italy officiated Germany's win over Argentina in 2014. In 2018, Néstor Pitana of Argentina refereed France's victory over Croatia — the first final played with VAR support.

In 2022, Poland's Szymon Marciniak became the first Polish referee to take a World Cup final, earning wide praise for his handling of Argentina's epic win over France, including correctly waving away a penalty appeal after spotting a dive in the French box. Looking further back, Morocco's Said Belqola refereed the 1998 final between Brazil and France, remaining the only African official — and the only Arab official — to referee the men's final. No referee from Asia has yet received the appointment, something Arab and Asian federations hope to see change.

The pattern is clear: European and South American referees dominate because their domestic and continental competitions supply most of the high-intensity matches FIFA uses for evaluation. The growing presence of referees from Asia, Africa, and North America in the later knockout rounds suggests that pattern may gradually widen.

The Road to the 2026 Final

The 2026 final is scheduled for July 19, 2026, at MetLife Stadium near New York, closing the largest World Cup ever staged: 48 teams, three host countries, and 104 matches. That scale means FIFA appointed the largest panel of match officials in the tournament's history, drawn from every confederation and supported by VAR at every game and semi-automated offside technology, which was first used at the 2022 World Cup in Qatar.

As of mid-July 2026, FIFA had not publicly confirmed who will referee the final. That is normal: the appointment traditionally lands only a few days before the match, once the two finalists are known and neutrality rules can be applied. Candidates are generally the officials who have come through the knockout rounds with the strongest assessment scores — experienced names from UEFA and CONMEBOL competitions, with elite referees from Asia and the Americas also in the wider conversation.

FAQ

Who chooses the World Cup final referee?

The FIFA Referees Committee, working with FIFA's Refereeing department, selects and appoints World Cup match officials. The final appointment is based on performance assessments across the tournament and is usually confirmed two to four days before the match.

Can the final referee come from a finalist's country?

No. FIFA's neutrality rules prevent a referee from officiating a match involving their own country, and the committee generally avoids appointing officials from the same confederation as a finalist when a suitable neutral referee is available.

Who refereed the 2022 World Cup final?

Szymon Marciniak of Poland refereed the 2022 final between Argentina and France at Lusail Stadium — the first Polish official to take charge of a World Cup final. His performance, including a correctly judged dive by Marcus Thuram, was widely praised.

When is the 2026 final referee announced?

FIFA usually confirms the final's officiating team only a few days before the match. The 2026 final is scheduled for July 19, 2026, at MetLife Stadium, so the appointment was expected in the days immediately before that date.

Does the final referee work with VAR?

Yes. Every World Cup match since 2018 has used the video assistant referee system, and the 2022 and 2026 tournaments added semi-automated offside technology. The final referee leads a full team: two assistants, a fourth official, and a VAR crew.
