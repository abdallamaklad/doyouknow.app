AFC Champions League Elite: Format, Saudi Clubs, and Records

Introduction

The AFC Champions League Elite is Asia's top club football competition and the successor to the AFC Champions League. Run by the Asian Football Confederation, it brings together the strongest clubs from across the continent — and since the rebrand took effect in the 2024–25 season, Saudi Arabia has sat at the center of the story, both as the competition's most successful football market and as host of its final rounds.

The rebrand was more than a new name. The AFC compressed the old 40-team group stage into a tighter, richer tournament with 24 teams, a Swiss-style league stage, higher prize money, and a centralized finals week in Jeddah. If you follow Saudi football, this is the competition that pits Al-Hilal, Al-Nassr, Al-Ahli and Al-Ittihad against the best of Japan, South Korea, Iran, Australia and beyond.

What Changed: From Group Stage to League Stage

Under the old AFC Champions League format, 40 clubs were split into ten groups of four across West and East Asia, playing six group matches each. The Elite cut the field to 24 teams: two league-stage tables of 12 clubs each, one for the West region and one for the East. Qualification is earned through domestic league and cup results, with slot allocations based on the AFC's club competitions ranking of member associations.

In the league stage, each club plays eight matches — four at home and four away — against eight different opponents drawn from within its own region. There are no traditional groups; all 12 teams in a region sit in one table, ranked by points, with goal difference and goals scored as tiebreakers. The format resembles the UEFA Champions League's league phase and guarantees more heavyweight matchups earlier in the season.

Another notable change is squad registration. The Elite relaxed foreign-player limits for the competition, which suits the Saudi Pro League's star-heavy rosters, and it raised prize money to record levels for Asian club football — the AFC announced a champion's cheque in the region of 12 million US dollars, several times the old figure.

How the Season Works

The league stage runs through the autumn and winter, after which the top eight clubs in each region advance to the round of 16. That round is played over two legs, home and away, in the spring, with the winners moving into the final stage.

From the quarter-finals onward, everything moves to one host. Saudi Arabia was awarded the first final stages, and Jeddah hosted the quarter-finals, semi-finals and final of the inaugural 2024–25 edition as single matches. The one-off format turned the last week of the tournament into a mini-festival: eight teams, three rounds, one city, and a champion crowned inside about ten days.

The calendar matters for fans planning travel. League-stage fixtures are spread across the season on midweek nights, the round of 16 arrives in early spring, and the finals cluster lands in late April and early May. Clubs that win the Elite also move on to FIFA's global club competitions, which is one reason the tournament carries weight far beyond Asia.

Saudi Clubs in Asia: The Record

Saudi clubs are the most decorated in Asian football. Al-Hilal holds the record with four continental titles (1991, 2000, 2019 and 2021) and remains the benchmark everyone measures against. Al-Nassr, despite enormous domestic success and the arrival of Cristiano Ronaldo, is still chasing a first Asian crown — the club's run to the 2025 semi-finals ended against Japan's Kawasaki Frontale.

Al-Ittihad won back-to-back AFC Champions League titles in 2004 and 2005, and Al-Ahli completed the set for Jeddah in May 2025 by winning the first Elite edition. Playing at home at King Abdullah Sports City, Al-Ahli beat Kawasaki Frontale 2–0 in the final with first-half goals from Galeno and Franck Kessié, both set up by Roberto Firmino, who was named the tournament's most valuable player. Al-Ahli finished the campaign unbeaten.

That 2025 final underlined the balance of power: three of the four semi-finalists in the first Elite season were Saudi (Al-Hilal, Al-Nassr and Al-Ahli). With Jeddah hosting the early final stages and Saudi clubs dominating the West region league table, the Kingdom's investment in football — part of the broader sports push around Riyadh Season and giga-projects such as Qiddiya — is now directly visible in Asian results. Why the Elite Matters

For Saudi football, the Elite is the annual exam. Domestic dominance is expected; Asian titles are how clubs are judged across the continent. The tournament also drives tourism and broadcasting interest into the Kingdom, especially in finals weeks when tens of thousands of traveling fans fill Jeddah.

For neutral fans, the format is simply easier to follow: one table per region, every match against a different opponent, and a single-venue sprint to the trophy. As the competition beds in, expect the West region race to keep featuring Saudi clubs at the top — and expect every other Asian giant to measure itself against them. Frequently Asked Questions

What is the AFC Champions League Elite?

It is Asia's premier club competition, renamed and reformatted from the old AFC Champions League starting with the 2024–25 season. It features 24 clubs in two regional league stages, followed by knockouts and a centralized finals week.

How many teams play in the league stage?

Twenty-four: twelve in the West region and twelve in the East. Each club plays eight matches against eight different opponents within its region, and the top eight in each table reach the round of 16.

Where is the AFC Champions League Elite final played?

From the quarter-finals onward, the first editions were centralized in Jeddah, Saudi Arabia, with all matches played as single games at venues including King Abdullah Sports City.

Which Saudi club has won the most Asian titles?

Al-Hilal, with four continental championships (1991, 2000, 2019 and 2021). Al-Ittihad won two (2004, 2005) and Al-Ahli won the first Elite title in 2025.

Who won the first AFC Champions League Elite?

Al-Ahli of Saudi Arabia won the inaugural 2024–25 edition, defeating Japan's Kawasaki Frontale 2–0 in the Jeddah final on 3 May 2025 to complete an unbeaten campaign.
