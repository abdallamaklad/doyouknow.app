Iqama in Saudi Arabia: Renewal, Fees, and Dependents

doyouknow.app Editorial Team2026-07-1017 min read

Editorial note: This guide was checked against official Saudi government sources and service portals on 2026-07-10. Fees, procedures, and eligibility rules can change, so confirm details through the official channels linked below before acting.

What Is the Iqama and Why It Matters

The iqama is the official residency permit issued to expatriates living and working in Saudi Arabia. It is a small plastic card, but its importance is enormous. Without a valid iqama, a resident cannot open most bank accounts, sign a rental contract, obtain a driving license, access government services, or leave and re-enter the country smoothly. Employers are responsible for sponsoring their employees and usually for initiating the iqama process, but residents still need to understand their own obligations.

The iqama contains key information: your full name, nationality, employer name, profession, iqama number, date of birth, and expiry date. It also carries a chip and QR code that authorities can scan. Because the iqama is tied to a sponsor, changing jobs or sponsors requires specific legal steps. The Saudi government has moved most iqama services online, so knowing how to use Absher and Muqeem saves time and reduces errors.

Keeping your iqama valid is a legal duty. Employers must issue or renew the iqama within 90 days of an employee's arrival or expiry. Employees should carry the card or keep a digital copy available. In this guide we cover the full lifecycle of the iqama, from first issuance through renewal, dependent additions, fee payments, and troubleshooting.

Who Needs an Iqama

Any non-Saudi citizen who intends to live in Saudi Arabia for work, family, or study purposes generally needs an iqama. Tourists and short-term visitors on an e-visa or visit visa do not need one. Business visitors with a commercial visit visa also remain outside the iqama system. However, anyone converting a visit visa to a work or residence status must apply for an iqama.

The employer is the sponsor for most work-related iqamas. For family members, the primary visa holder—usually the father or husband—acts as the sponsor. Domestic workers receive iqamas sponsored by their sponsoring family. Students on government scholarships may have iqamas sponsored by the scholarship authority. Each category has slightly different document requirements, but the core process is similar: medical test, insurance payment, sponsor registration, fingerprinting, and card printing.

Children born in Saudi Arabia to expatriate parents do not automatically receive Saudi citizenship. They are added to the father's iqama as dependents and need their own dependent iqama once they reach the age determined by current regulations. Understanding sponsorship rules helps families avoid last-minute problems when children approach the dependent age limit.

How to Apply for an Iqama for the First Time

The first-time iqama process starts after the employee enters Saudi Arabia on a work visit visa. The employer begins by issuing a work permit through the Ministry of Human Resources and Social Development. After the work permit is approved, the employee undergoes a medical examination at an approved medical center. The medical check screens for infectious diseases and conditions that may affect public health.

Once the medical result is uploaded, the employer pays the required fees through Absher or a government payment channel such as SADAD. The next step is fingerprinting and biometrics at a Jawazat office or authorized center. The employee should bring the passport, visa copy, photos, and any forms requested by the sponsor. After biometrics are recorded, the sponsor can request iqama issuance through the Muqeem system.

The iqama is usually printed within a few days and delivered to the employer. New residents should check all details on the card carefully. Errors in name spelling, profession, or date of birth can cause problems later. If mistakes are found, the sponsor should request a correction through Absher or Jawazat before the card is used for other services.

How to Renew Your Iqama

Iqama renewal is normally handled by the sponsor before the expiry date. The standard validity period is one year, although some iqamas may be issued for shorter periods depending on the visa type or company status. Renewal requires three main conditions: the work permit must be valid or renewed, the resident must have valid medical insurance, and any outstanding fees or fines must be paid.

The sponsor logs into Absher, selects the employee, and initiates the renewal request. The system checks whether the medical insurance policy covers the renewal period. If the insurance is missing or expired, a new policy must be purchased from an approved provider. After insurance verification, the sponsor pays the renewal fee. The renewed iqama can often be downloaded digitally or collected as a physical card.

Residents should track their iqama expiry date carefully. Absher sends reminders, but relying solely on the employer can be risky. If the iqama expires, the resident may face daily fines, difficulties at banks, and problems leaving the country. In cases where the employer delays renewal, the resident can raise a complaint through the Ministry of Human Resources and Social Development or transfer sponsorship if eligible.

Iqama Fees and Who Pays Them

Iqama fees are set by the Saudi government and are usually paid by the employer as part of the employment contract. The main fees include the iqama issuance or renewal fee, the work permit fee, and the medical insurance premium. Additional charges may apply for dependent fees, replacement of lost cards, and late renewal penalties. Exact amounts change from time to time, so check the official fee schedule on the Jawazat or Absher portals.

Dependent fees are charged annually for each family member sponsored by a resident. The fee is typically paid when the resident renews their own iqama or the dependent iqama. Many employers do not cover dependent fees, so expatriates should budget for this cost. Some companies include dependent fees in the salary package for senior employees, but this should be confirmed in writing.

Late renewal penalties can accumulate quickly. If an iqama expires, a fine may be charged for each day of delay, up to a maximum amount. Employers who fail to renew multiple employees may face additional sanctions. Residents who discover an expired iqama should contact their sponsor immediately and, if necessary, seek help from the relevant government authorities.

Adding and Managing Dependents

Expatriates who meet the salary and profession requirements can sponsor their spouse and children as dependents. The process starts in Absher by submitting a dependent visa application. Once the visa is issued, the family member travels to Saudi Arabia and undergoes a medical test. After the medical result is approved and dependent fees are paid, the dependent iqama is issued.

The profession listed on the sponsor's iqama plays an important role. Some professions are not eligible to sponsor dependents under current rules. The salary requirement also changes from time to time. Sponsors should verify the latest criteria on the Jawazat website or through Absher before starting the application. Attempting to apply without meeting the requirements leads to rejection and wasted fees.

Dependent iqamas must be renewed along with the sponsor's iqama. If a dependent leaves Saudi Arabia permanently, the sponsor should cancel the dependent iqama to stop future fees. Newborn children must be added to the father's iqama within the timeframe set by Jawazat. Keeping dependent records up to date protects the family's legal status and avoids fines.

Using Absher and Muqeem for Iqama Services

Most iqama services are now digital. Absher is the main platform for residents and sponsors to manage iqama details, pay fees, update passports, and request transfers. Muqeem is the employer-facing system used to issue, renew, and cancel iqamas. Both systems are linked to the National Information Center.

Residents should register for an Absher account as soon as they receive their iqama. Registration requires a mobile number, iqama number, and sometimes a visit to an Absher activation kiosk. Once activated, the account allows users to check expiry dates, pay traffic fines, book appointments, and update personal information. Employers manage Muqeem access through the company's authorized representatives.

Digital receipts and iqama copies are accepted by many banks and government offices. However, some procedures still require a physical visit to Jawazat or a service center. Residents should keep both digital and printed copies of their iqama and renewal receipts. If an Absher account is locked, it can usually be unlocked through the mobile app or at a service center.

Common Iqama Problems and How to Fix Them

One common problem is a delayed renewal by the employer. If the sponsor does not renew the iqama on time, the resident should first request action in writing. If the delay continues, the Ministry of Human Resources and Social Development offers channels to report violations. In some cases, the resident may be able to transfer sponsorship to a new employer.

Lost or damaged iqamas can be replaced by paying a replacement fee and submitting a request through Absher or the sponsor's Muqeem account. Name mismatches between the iqama and passport can cause travel problems; these should be corrected by Jawazat before booking flights. Profession mismatches can affect eligibility for driving licenses or dependent sponsorship.

Another issue is an iqama blocked due to unpaid traffic fines or government fees. The block is usually removed automatically after payment, but it may take a few hours to update. If the block persists, residents can contact the relevant authority with the payment receipt. Keeping a clean record and monitoring Absher notifications prevents most of these problems.

Compliance, Penalties, and Final Tips

Saudi Arabia takes residency compliance seriously. Employers who fail to issue or renew iqamas can be fined and may lose the ability to sponsor new workers. Residents with expired iqamas may face fines, detention, deportation, or bans from re-entry. Working for an employer other than your sponsor without proper transfer is illegal and can lead to severe penalties.

The best way to stay compliant is to monitor expiry dates, keep documents updated, and communicate with the sponsor early. Residents should not wait until the last day to renew. They should also understand their rights: employers are generally responsible for iqama costs and renewal fees unless the contract states otherwise.

Before leaving Saudi Arabia for a long trip, confirm that your iqama and re-entry visa are valid. Some residents have been stranded at airports because their iqama expired while they were abroad. A few minutes of checking in Absher can prevent a costly and stressful situation. For complex cases, consulting a licensed immigration service agent or the Jawazat helpline is a wise step.

Iqama Address, Absher, and Keeping Details Updated

Your iqama is linked to your registered address in Absher. When you move to a new apartment, you should update your address through the Absher portal or mobile app. Keeping the address current is important because government notifications, court summons, and some banking correspondence may be tied to it. An updated address is also helpful when applying for a family visit visa or dependent iqama, because authorities want to confirm where the sponsor lives.

Absher also stores your mobile number and email address. If you change your phone number, update it in Absher as soon as possible. Many government services send one-time passwords to the registered number, and losing access can lock you out of important transactions. Some residents visit an Absher kiosk or Jawazat office to verify their identity when making major profile changes.

Regularly review your Absher dashboard for alerts about expiry dates, unpaid fines, or required updates. A few minutes of maintenance each month prevents urgent problems later. If you notice incorrect information, ask your sponsor to correct it through Muqeem or contact Jawazat directly.

Exit and Re-Entry Visas

If you plan to leave Saudi Arabia temporarily, you need a valid exit and re-entry visa unless you are traveling on a final exit visa. The sponsor usually applies for the exit and re-entry visa through Absher or Muqeem. The visa is valid for a limited period, and you must return before it expires.

The cost of an exit and re-entry visa varies based on duration and whether it is single or multiple entry. Multiple-entry visas cost more but allow several trips. The fee is paid through SADAD. Once issued, the visa is linked electronically to your passport and iqama. Airlines can verify it at check-in.

If your circumstances change and you cannot return before the visa expires, your sponsor may be able to extend it while you are abroad, depending on current rules. If the visa expires while you are outside Saudi Arabia, you may need a new visa to return. Always confirm the expiry date before booking flights.

Changing Employers and Sponsorship Transfer

Saudi labor rules allow workers to transfer sponsorship to a new employer under certain conditions. This is sometimes called tanazul or sponsorship transfer. The process requires the current sponsor’s agreement, unless the worker has a valid reason for a transfer without consent, such as unpaid wages or contract violations. The Ministry of Human Resources and Social Development handles disputes and approvals.

The new employer initiates the transfer request through Qiwa or the relevant labor system. Once approved, Jawazat updates the iqama to show the new sponsor. The employee does not receive a new plastic card immediately, but the electronic record changes. Some professions and visa categories may have restrictions on transfer.

Before agreeing to a transfer, review your new contract, salary, benefits, and job title. Make sure the new sponsor will renew your iqama on time. A transfer can be a good opportunity, but it is important to understand the terms clearly.

Iqama Cancellation and Final Exit

When a resident leaves Saudi Arabia permanently, the sponsor must issue a final exit visa and cancel the iqama. The final exit visa gives the resident a limited window to leave the country. After departure, the iqama is no longer valid, and the resident cannot return on that iqama.

Before applying for final exit, the sponsor should settle any outstanding debts, fines, or legal issues related to the employee. The employee should close bank accounts if required, settle utility bills, and cancel subscriptions. Some residents also need to obtain a salary certificate or end-of-service benefits from the employer.

If a resident leaves without a proper final exit, they may face legal problems or a ban on re-entering Saudi Arabia. Employers who fail to cancel an iqama after an employee departs may also face penalties. Both parties should follow the official process carefully.

Iqama Renewal Checklist and Calendar

Staying organized makes iqama management much easier. Create a calendar reminder at least 60 days before the expiry date. Ask your sponsor to confirm that your work permit, medical insurance, and any dependent records are up to date. Check Absher for unpaid traffic fines or government fees that could block renewal.

Keep a folder—physical or digital—with copies of your passport, iqama, visa, medical insurance card, and renewal receipts. Share the renewal timeline with family members who depend on your sponsorship. If you have children approaching the dependent age limit, research their options well in advance.

After renewal, verify that the new expiry date appears correctly in Absher and on the physical card. Report any errors immediately. A small amount of planning each quarter saves stress and money over the long term.

Iqama and Banking

Banks in Saudi Arabia require a valid iqama to open most accounts and obtain credit cards. When you open an account, the bank records your iqama number and links it to your profile. If your iqama expires, the bank may freeze your account or limit transactions until you provide a renewed copy. This is why it is important to update your bank records immediately after renewal.

Some banks allow non-residents or visitors to open limited accounts, but full banking services such as loans, credit cards, and investment accounts require residency. Salary transfers from employers also require a valid iqama. Most employers ask for your bank IBAN when setting up payroll.

If you change jobs and your sponsor changes, inform your bank. Some banks require updated employment information for credit products. Keeping your contact details and iqama copy current prevents interruptions in online banking and card services.

Iqama and Healthcare Access

Your iqama is linked to your medical insurance policy and healthcare records. When you visit a hospital or clinic, staff may ask for your iqama and insurance card. Without valid insurance, you may have to pay full costs for treatment, which can be expensive. Employers are required to provide medical insurance for employees, but dependents must be added separately.

The Council of Health Insurance sets minimum coverage standards. Policies must cover emergency treatment, outpatient visits, and prescribed medications according to the policy terms. Check which hospitals and clinics are in your insurance network before seeking treatment.

When your iqama is renewed, your insurance must also be renewed for the same period. A gap in coverage can cause claim rejections. Keep your insurance card and policy number with you, and update your insurance information in Absher and with healthcare providers.

Iqama for Students and Scholarship Holders

Students who come to Saudi Arabia on government scholarships receive iqamas sponsored by the scholarship authority or their university. The process is similar to work iqamas but the sponsor is an educational institution rather than an employer. Students must maintain enrollment and good academic standing to keep their iqama valid.

Scholarship students often have access to university health clinics and housing. Their dependents, if allowed, are sponsored under the student’s iqama subject to financial and academic conditions. Any interruption in studies can affect residency status, so students should communicate with their scholarship office before making major decisions.

After graduation, students who find employment must transfer their sponsorship to the new employer before starting work. The transfer process follows the same rules as other workers. Planning the transition early prevents gaps in legal status.

Iqama and Legal Disputes

Legal disputes can affect your iqama status. If you are involved in a court case or have outstanding judgments, your iqama may be blocked from renewal or exit. Resolving the dispute and obtaining clearance from the court is necessary before normal services resume.

Labor disputes with employers are handled by the Ministry of Human Resources and Social Development. If an employer fails to renew your iqama or pay wages, file a complaint through the ministry’s channels. The ministry can issue rulings that allow you to transfer sponsorship or receive owed compensation.

Always keep copies of court documents, labor complaints, and official correspondence. These records are essential if you need to explain your situation to Jawazat or a new sponsor.

Renewing Iqama During Company Changes

If your employer changes its legal name, merges with another company, or closes a branch, your iqama records may need updating. The sponsor should notify Jawazat and update the company information in Muqeem. Delays in updating company records can block employee renewals.

When a company closes entirely, employees may need to transfer sponsorship quickly. The Ministry of Human Resources and Social Development can provide guidance in such cases. Workers should not wait until their iqama expires to address company problems.

Regularly check Absher to confirm that your sponsor’s name matches your current employer. If you see an old or unfamiliar company name, ask your HR department for clarification. Keeping records aligned prevents unexpected renewal blocks.

Frequently asked questions

What is an iqama?

It is the official residency permit for expatriates living and working in Saudi Arabia.

Who is responsible for renewing my iqama?

Your employer or sponsor is normally responsible, but you should monitor the expiry date and remind them.

Can I renew my iqama myself?

The renewal request is usually submitted by the sponsor through Absher or Muqeem, not by the employee directly.

How much are iqama fees?

Fees include iqama renewal, work permit, medical insurance, and possibly dependent fees. Check Absher or Jawazat for current amounts.

Can I sponsor my family on my iqama?

Yes, if your profession and salary meet the current requirements set by Jawazat.

What happens if my iqama expires?

You may face daily fines, service blocks, and travel restrictions. Contact your sponsor immediately.

How do I replace a lost iqama?

Report the loss and request a replacement through your sponsor or Absher, paying the replacement fee.
