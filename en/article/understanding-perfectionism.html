Understanding Perfectionism and How to Manage It

    • Introduction

    Perfectionism is a personality trait defined by setting excessively high standards paired with harsh self-criticism when those standards are not met — it is distinct from simply valuing quality or working hard.

    Psychologists generally describe two components: striving for high standards, and a more corrosive tendency toward self-critical evaluation that persists even after real achievements.

    Why Perfectionism Can Backfire

    Research links high self-critical perfectionism with increased anxiety, depression, procrastination, and burnout, partly because the bar for 'success' keeps moving and mistakes feel identity-threatening rather than simply informative.

    Perfectionism can also reduce output rather than improve it: fear of an imperfect result often leads to avoidance or over-revision, delaying or preventing work from being finished at all.

    How to Manage Perfectionist Tendencies

    Common approaches include setting 'good enough' thresholds in advance for lower-stakes tasks, deliberately submitting imperfect work to build tolerance for it, and separating self-worth from performance outcomes.

    Cognitive behavioral techniques — identifying and challenging all-or-nothing thinking about success and failure — are frequently used in therapy to reduce the anxiety perfectionism tends to generate.

    FAQ

    Is perfectionism always bad?

    Not entirely — striving for high standards can drive achievement, but research shows the self-critical component of perfectionism is the part most consistently linked to anxiety, depression, and burnout.

    What is the difference between perfectionism and having high standards?

    High standards paired with self-compassion when falling short is generally healthy; perfectionism adds harsh self-judgment and a sense that anything short of flawless equals failure.

    Can perfectionism cause procrastination?

    Yes. Fear of producing an imperfect result is one of the most common drivers of procrastination, since avoiding the task avoids the risk of falling short.
